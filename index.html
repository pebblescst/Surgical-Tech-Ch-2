Key concept
The OR is engineered for infection control, environmental safety, and efficient use of personnel, time, space, and materials
Primary accreditation body for all healthcare facilities: The Joint Commission (TJC) — www.jointcommission.org
Regulatory Agencies & Standards
APIC
Association for Professionals in Infection Control and Epidemiology — sets infection prevention standards
AHRQ
Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality — federal agency; evidence-based quality and safety guidelines
AIA
American Institute of Architects — OR design and construction standards for healthcare facilities
EPA
Environmental Protection Agency — governs disposal of medical/hazardous waste from healthcare facilities
OSHA
Occupational Safety and Health Administration — protects healthcare workers from occupational hazards (bloodborne pathogens, chemical exposure, ergonomics)
Accreditation: A team of professionals evaluates a healthcare institution's practices, policies, and outcomes of patient care. TJC is the primary accreditation organization for all healthcare facilities.
Principles of Operating Room Design
Infection control
Physical layout, airflow, surface materials, and traffic patterns all designed to minimize contamination and HAI risk
Environmental safety
Safe handling of electricity, gases, radiation, and hazardous materials; protection for patients and all OR personnel
Efficient use of resources
Optimize use of personnel, time, space, and material resources to support efficient patient throughput and care quality
Essential OR Equipment & Furniture
Operating table
Adjustable surgical table; positions patient for optimal surgical access; multiple configurations for different procedures
Back table
Large sterile draped table; holds the full sterile instrument and supply setup; managed by the STSR
Mayo stand
Small mobile instrument stand positioned over the patient; holds frequently used instruments within immediate reach of the surgeon and scrub tech
Kick bucket
Mobile bucket on wheels; holds soiled sponges and debris; kicked with foot to reposition without breaking sterility
Ring stand
Circular basin stand; holds sterile basins of solutions (irrigation fluid, saline) for the sterile field
Airflow, Lighting, Gases & Electricity
Airflow & ventilation
Strictly regulated to reduce airborne particles carrying infectious microorganisms. Positive-pressure ventilation in ORs — air flows outward to prevent contaminants entering. HEPA filtration used.
Lighting
Multiple light sources used in the OR; overhead surgical lights provide shadow-free illumination of the operative field; headlights, fiberoptic, and task lighting also used
Compressed gases
Various compressed gases used as adjuncts to anesthesia (O₂, N₂O, air) and as power sources for pneumatic surgical instruments (drills, saws)
Electricity
Multiple outlets for equipment (electrosurgical units, lights, monitors, pumps); isolated power systems and line isolation monitors prevent electric shock hazards
Special Procedure Rooms
Cystoscopy room Interventional radiology Nuclear medicine department Emergency department
These rooms are designed for specific procedures that require specialized equipment, shielding (radiation), or rapid access. Surgical technologists may be required to work in any of these settings.
Surgical Suite Work Areas
🏢 Surgical offices
Administrative and management space for OR scheduling, staffing, and coordination
🚪 Locker room / Lounge
Staff changing area and rest space; surgical attire donned here; semi-restricted zone transition
🛏 Preoperative patient care area
Patient holding/prep area; consent verification, IV placement, pre-op assessment, anesthesia consultation
🚿 Scrub sinks
Hand and forearm surgical scrub sinks located just outside OR entry; used by all scrubbed team members before gowning and gloving
📦 Sterile instrument room
Restricted storage area for sterilized instrument sets, packaged supplies, and implants; maintained as a clean, organized, temperature-controlled space
⭕ Central core
The innermost restricted corridor or hub of the surgical suite; minimizes traffic; provides direct access to sterile storage and ORs; highest level of restriction
🗃 Equipment storage
Dedicated space for OR equipment (tables, positioning devices, specialty equipment); organized for efficient retrieval
🔴 Utility workroom & decontamination
Soiled utility room; post-case instrument decontamination begins here; separated from clean areas; negative-pressure ventilation; PPE required
✅ Clean processing area
Where instruments are inspected, reassembled, and packaged for sterilization; separated from decontamination area
💉 Anesthesia department
Anesthesia equipment storage, drug preparation, and provider workspace; connects to ORs and pre-op holding
🛌 PACU
Post-Anesthesia Care Unit; patients recover from anesthesia here; monitored until stable for discharge to floor or home
👨‍👩‍👧 Surgery waiting area
Family/patient waiting; communication point between OR staff and patient families; located in unrestricted zone
Also note: Integrated OR systems — advanced ORs with digital integration of imaging, equipment controls, and communication technology built into the room infrastructure.
OR Traffic Zones — Restriction Levels
Unrestricted zone
Waiting areas, corridors, administrative offices; street clothes permitted; patients, families, and visitors may be present
Semi-restricted zone
Support areas (instrument storage, corridors leading to ORs); OR attire required; traffic limited to authorized personnel
Restricted zone
ORs, scrub sink areas, sterile core; OR attire + surgical mask required; only authorized personnel with valid reason to be present
Hospital Ancillary Departments & Functions
Pathology
Analyzes tissue specimens, biopsies, and cultures; intraoperative frozen sections for rapid diagnosis; identifies cancer margins
Nuclear medicine & interventional radiology
Diagnostic and therapeutic procedures using radioactive tracers and image-guided techniques; STs may work in IR suites
Infection control
Monitors HAI rates; investigates outbreaks; enforces infection prevention policies; partners with surgical services
Biomedical engineering
Maintains, repairs, and certifies all medical equipment; ensures equipment safety and function before surgical use
Materials management
Procurement, distribution, and inventory management of all supplies and equipment used in the facility
Central supply
Receives, processes, and distributes sterile supplies to all departments; closely linked with sterile processing
Pharmacy
Prepares and dispenses medications; supports OR drug needs including anesthesia drugs, irrigation solutions, and hemostatic agents
Laboratory
Blood tests, cultures, pathology processing, urinalysis; provides intraoperative lab values (ABGs, coagulation studies)
Blood bank
Stores, cross-matches, and distributes blood products; critical for surgical cases with hemorrhage risk
Risk management
Identifies, analyzes, and mitigates risks to patients, staff, and institution; handles incident reports and liability
Medical records
Maintains patient health records; ensures documentation accuracy, privacy, and regulatory compliance (HIPAA)
Environmental services
OR housekeeping and terminal cleaning between and after cases; critical for infection control
Also: Communication systems, Facilities maintenance, Security, Nutritional services — all support the OR environment and surgical patient's perioperative journey.

Health Care Management Structure
Board of directors
Highest governing body; sets institutional mission, policies, and strategic direction
Medical staff
Licensed physicians and providers; governed by medical staff bylaws; accountable to board
Administration
Day-to-day operational management; implements board directives; manages departments and staff
Chain of command: Defines the reporting relationship between management and staff. Each employee reports to their immediate line manager. Critical for conflict resolution, incident reporting, and patient safety escalation.
Types of Ambulatory Surgery Facilities
Freestanding facility
Independently owned and operated ambulatory surgery center (ASC); physically separate from a hospital; serves elective outpatient surgical cases; may be physician-owned
Integrated hospital-based outpatient
Outpatient surgical services fully integrated within the main hospital building and surgical department; shares staff and resources with inpatient ORs
Self-contained hospital outpatient facility
Separate dedicated outpatient surgery unit within or adjacent to the hospital campus; distinct staffing and scheduling from inpatient OR; shares hospital resources
Office-based outpatient surgery
Minor surgical procedures performed in a physician's office setting; typically for minor procedures under local anesthesia; least regulated of the four types
Advantages of Ambulatory Surgery
Patient acceptance
Patients prefer same-day surgery; less disruption to daily life; reduced hospital stay anxiety
Cost effectiveness
Lower overhead than inpatient hospital surgery; reduces overall healthcare costs for payers and patients
Ease of scheduling
More flexible scheduling; less competition with emergent/inpatient cases; predictable case flow
Control over environment
Greater control over surgical environment, supply standardization, and equipment preferences
Efficiency
Streamlined workflows; faster turnover times; focused scope of procedures optimizes team performance
Disadvantages of Ambulatory Surgery
Space & staffing limitations
May lack sufficient physical space or trained staff to handle complex cases or emergencies
Expanded responsibilities
Staff may be responsible for areas not usually assigned in a larger facility (e.g., sterilization, supply, equipment management)
Training requirements
May require special or updated staff training to ensure standards are met; less specialized staff available
Medical-legal exposure
Increased medical-legal responsibilities without the infrastructure of a full hospital risk management department
Lack of centralized support
Limited or no access to specialized support services (ICU, blood bank, specialist backup) if complications arise
No skilled care after discharge
No skilled nursing available after patient goes home; relies on patient/caregiver compliance with post-op instructions
Surgeon
Licensed physician who performs the surgical procedure; leads the sterile team; ultimately responsible for patient care decisions in the OR
Assistant surgeon
Provides surgical assistance to the primary surgeon; may be another surgeon, surgical resident, PA, CSFA, or advanced practice provider
Anesthesia provider
Anesthesiologist (MD/DO) or Certified Registered Nurse Anesthetist (CRNA); manages patient's anesthesia, airway, and hemodynamic status throughout the procedure
Perioperative registered nurse (RN)
Serves as circulating nurse; manages non-sterile aspects of the OR; responsible for patient assessment, documentation, and coordination; licensed scope of practice varies by state
Certified Surgical Technologist (CST)
Scrub role (STSR) — prepares and maintains sterile field; passes instruments; directly assists surgeon; may assist in circulator role per state scope
Physician assistant (PA)
May serve as first assistant in surgery; licensed prescriber; scope varies by supervising physician agreement and state law
Licensed practical nurse (LPN)
May assist in perioperative care; limited scope compared to RN; supportive role in holding area or circulating functions under RN supervision
Certified Surgical First Assistant (CSFA)
Advanced CST; provides direct patient care during surgery as first assistant; requires additional certification beyond CST; works under surgeon supervision
Nonphysician surgical assistant (NPSA)
Provides first-assistant surgical care without physician licensure; scope defined by state law and institutional credentialing
Certified anesthesia technologist
Supports the anesthesia provider; prepares and maintains anesthesia equipment; manages anesthesia supplies and drugs under supervision
Operating room educator
Provides ongoing staff education; orients new employees; coordinates competency assessments and in-service training for OR personnel
Operating room assistant (ORA)
Unlicensed assistive personnel (UAP); performs support tasks (patient transport, equipment setup, OR cleaning) under supervision
Sterile processing professionals
Decontaminate, inspect, reassemble, package, and sterilize surgical instruments; critical link in preventing surgical site infections
Radiology technician
Operates intraoperative imaging equipment (C-arm fluoroscopy, portable X-ray); assists with intraoperative imaging guidance
EEG technician
Monitors intraoperative neurophysiological signals (EEG, evoked potentials) during neurosurgery or spine procedures to detect nerve injury in real time
Medical industry representative
Company representative present during procedures to provide technical support for specialty implants, instruments, or devices; not a licensed care provider
Cardiovascular perfusionist
Operates the cardiopulmonary bypass (heart-lung) machine during open-heart surgery; manages extracorporeal circulation
Director of surgical services / OR manager
Oversees the entire surgical department; responsible for staffing, budgets, compliance, and quality improvement
Unit clerk / Secretary
Manages OR scheduling, patient records, communication, and administrative support for the surgical department
